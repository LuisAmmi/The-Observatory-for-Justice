Who do you call when people who should protect you are the abusers?

The magazine

Founded in 2022, The Observer for Justice is a monthly magazine that reports about forms of iniquity and violence which victimize social communities all over the world. Our motto is ‘Fight injustice trough information’ and we decide to devote our first release to 20 years of chronic police abuse: from the 27th G8 summit, held in Genoa in 2001, marked by violent clashes between anti-globalization protesters and police, to the "Black Lives Matter" movement, boomed after the murder of George Floyd and other episodes of racial hatred perpetuated by white officers.

The issue

In the Issue section, you will find three selected insights on the topic. The first one is a detailed and unvarnished chronicle of what happened during those painful days of Genoa, due to which the European Court of Human Rights found Italy guilty of torture. The second is a recent analysis on data about racial bias and police brutality in United States. The last insight is a scientific article that highlight how intense and numerically significant are the contacts between police and people suffering from mental disorders.

The project

The Observatory for Justice is a project realized for the examination of the course of Information modelling and web technologies held by Fabio Vitali within the Master Degree in Digital Humanities and Digital Knowledge of the University of Bologna. According to the guideline, the purpose of the website is to display three articles dealing with the same theme/main topic. The scopes of the site are displaying articles through different styles and layouts which should be influenced by real typographic movements, achievable through a cross-analysis of textual metadata. So, let's explore the different styling taste from the past. Thanks to the style timeline, in the Filter section of the navbar, the user can access to this first number of the magazine switching from a very basic and contemporary style to one inspired by the Dada movement of 1920 and to 70’s style, shaped on the famous “Topolino” comic book (“Mickey Mouse” in English).

Documentation

Markup

The structure of the three selected article reflects a hierarchical form, composed of nested HTML elements, tags and a attributes. I added classes and specific id to better shape the diffent styles according to the elements of the page. Some complex characters and diacritical symbols have been repleaced by their respective marks to assure a correct reading by the HTML language. I also semantically marked up the article, in order to create a filtering feature for highlighting meaningful elements in respect to the central issue. With this purpose, I individuated mention persons, places, events, institution or companies, general concepts and different types of aggression perpetuated by policeman, mental disorders and ethnic group. Each element was therefore assigned to one or more classes, and detectable by the attribute "about", which we used to collect all elements of the same class and value, linking them to the checkboxes of our metadata panel to guarantee an efficient search.

Styles

Both the chosen styles - Dada and 1970s- are inspired by specific sources, that well symbolize the stylistic evolution and the design trends experienced in those years. The process of selection of the reference sources, the fonts, the layouts and colors is explained in details in the following subsections.

Dadaism

To reconstruct the 1920 historical style, I decide to take inspiration from the Dadaism movements and their styles. Developed in reaction to World War I, Dadaism is one of the most unconventional and Avante-Garde art and cultural movements of recent history, born in Zürich, Switzerland, at the Cabaret Voltaire (c. 1916) and lasted until the mid 1920s. Dadaism was a very eclectic movement that explored a range of non-traditional materials, nonsensical content and satire. Beyond typically artistic media, Dada artists also investigated performance art and literature. In their magazine and manifestos, they experimented with liberating the written word from institutional conventions. The movement reached various European and American cultural centers: among the most famous were the German, the Parisian and the New Yorker ones. They share aspects, but they also differ a lot. This is why choosing just one main reference magazine seemed to me the only chance to obtain a realistic dada style, faithful to the original example.

The reference magazine

The International Dada Archive, a scholarly resource for the study of the historic Dada movement, has been a precious support to reconstruct the Dadaist panorama and its testimonies. Going through its catalogue of periodicals, I found the Berlin-based Der DADA, a magazine edited by Raoul Hausmann, John Heartfield and George Grosz. It had only three releases: the first published in June 1919, the second in December 1919 and the third and last in April 1920.

It seemed to me full of details and interesting graphic cues, perfectly suitable to the digital format of a web page. Initially, I was undecided whether to use Der Dada or Dada magazine, born in Zurich in 1917 from the idea of Tristan Tzara, its editor, and in operation until 1920, when its head-quarter moved to Paris. Finally, the coherence of some visual aspect (as the quite homogeneous background color of the sheets of paper), harmoniously merged with graphic distortions, in Der Dada persuaded me. Thus, I decided to follow Raoul Hausmann’s trail also to design the cover image of the homepage, substitutive of the video footage, published by Rai and visible in the opening section of the website. The collage was inspired by his work “ABCD” (1923-1924), described by The Centre Pompidou of Paris, in this way: “This photomontage – a technique Raoul Hausmann started using in 1918 – exploits the many possibilities of collage. ABCD is a demonstration of the artist’s mastery of the art, the figures, objects, letters and numbers all being tightly integrated. The first four letters of the alphabet appear in the artist’s open mouth, adding a sense of sound to the purely visual, a sense supported by the scattered letters that form the word “VOCE”, voice. This produces a kind of still of a film with sound in which everything takes place at once.” I tried to replicate this sense of sound, quite significant compared to the silence to which George Flyod was force by white officer Derek Chauvin and to the scream of revenge “No Justice, No Peace” that resounded in the streets during the subsequent demonstrations. Thanks to PhotoScapeX and remove.bg, I cut out and put together pieces of photos and excerpts from newspapers, portraying the protests in Genoa during G8 Summit in 2001 and the ones broke out in USA during the summer of 2020. Finally, I overlaid the collage with some light effects in order to obtain an old-fashioned flavor. This is the result.
For what concerns the icon in the navbar to switch to Dada style, I opted for the Fountain, (New York, 1917) Duchamp’s first experiment with readymade and one among the more recognizable symbols of the movement.
I took a picture of the object and working with contrast value, I extracted the silhouette of the urinal.

Fonts

I mixed several fonts, in order to hold the variety of typographic styles that coexisted in Dada newspapers. I played especially with the headers and titles. Some opensource softwares, such as Whatthefont and MyFonts , have been very helpful to match the exact fonts of some pieces of text extracted by Der Dada pages. Anyway, lots of results produced by their algorithms lead to paid packages. I settled on a compromise between high resemblance and free downloadable options. For the general body (both in the Homepage and in the Issue page), I adopted Romana BT font. For the hyperlinks, I enlarged the letter-spacing to 10px, switching to Arial font-family, in order to invoke the non-linear Dada writing system.
Then, thanks to different id's, I modified titles and subtitles in the css file, experimenting different sizes, fonts and text decorations. Below some examples.
I also replicated some stylistic features of DADA magazine and other official publications. The style of “The Designer”, at the end of the homepage, want to be a freely inspired replica of the title of Dadaglobe solicitation form letter signed by Francis Picabia, Tristan Tzara, Georges Ribemont-Dessaignes and Walter Serner in November 1920.

Layout

Working on the layout was definitely the most funny part of the project. For all the wallpapers, in particular, I preferred to work on the creation of new images, rather than re-using pictures downloadeable from the internet. This has guaranteed me a not-discounted image uniformity, useful for a wide and not fixed homepage, without falling into the static of a background-color. Starting from the colors detected in the pages of Dada magazines, I created backgrounds with PhotoScapeX, on which I applied graphic disturbances such as noise reduction or some pieces of text applied with a high opacity.
I played with the borders: there are several black solid borders that frame some paragraph or images, toghether with some double borders, such as the one around the Issue's articles, and other dotted or red colored examples, scattered here and there, but always in a manner consistent with the meaning of the textual or visual content. For some sub-paragraphs, I exploited the "text-transform" function of css to rotate and translate portions of text, or the "writing-mode" function to give the sentences an unconventional direction. Comic book of 1970s

The 1970s were an eclectic decade filled with fresh impetus. The year 68 saw the opening of a new chapter involving the countries of the West and the Middle East. They are the years of Queen, of free love, of the landing of the first man on the moon, of the withdrawal of the American troops from Vietnam and of the explosion of the war of the Kippur. In 1975 Microsoft was founded, the process of democratisation of Spain started (after the death of dictator Francisco Franco) and in Italy there was the historical compromise between DC and PCI. In 1978, the Basaglia law was promulgated reforming psychiatric hospitals, the 'Brigate Rosse' shake the country. These are years of profound change. Still, even in those days, there are forms of art and entertainment that seem to survive any cultural revolution, although they were never deaf to such changes, continuing to enchant their audience, which grows from generation to generation. This is the case of Topolino, born from the homonymous American Mickey Mouse, created in 1928 by Walt Disney, who became a character of the famous comic from the 1940s and exported all over the world

The reference magazine

Topolino made its debut in Italy on 30 March 1930 in the weekly Illustrazione del Popolo, supplement of the Turin newspaper Gazzetta del Popolo. In 1935, the title was acquired by Arnoldo Mondadori Editore, but because of restrictions imposed by the fascist regime, the events of Topolino were censored. In 1949, to get out of the crisis in the sector, Mondadori introduced a change in format and frequency. From the weekly, the insertions Topolino became at first monthly, but at last in the form of a booklet, then later resumed the weekly periodicity. It is at the turn of the 50s that the first Topolino made in Italy debuted, which not only transposed the stories of the American homonym for the national public, but invented new ones. Since the 1960s, stories of Italian origin became predominant thanks to authors such as Romano Scarpa, considered one of the greatest Disney authors ever. In 1970, the Republic of San Marino issued the first stamp in the world dedicated to the character Disney. From the '70s, strengthened by the classic adventures of Topolino, designed by the great authors of the first generation, the new masters such as Massimo De Vita, Giorgio Pezzin and Bruno Concina separated the Italian Topolino from the American model. They moved Topolino, made a little more spiritual and ironic, into a wide variety of adventures and scavenger hunts. This is why I decided to take as main reference Topolino magazine of '70s'. In short, while the adult world is at the mercy of great revolutions, the teenage world clings to the adventures of a mouse and his crew of friends. It's through this generation of girls and boys, some of whom became admirers and collectors, that the 1970 style of this project came into being. Below, a few issues of the magazine, in order of release (1970-1979).
The universe of the Topolino comic book, however, did not end with the story of the adventures of the protagonists, but extended to a large amount of advertising inserts of various kinds, which represented an interesting photograph of the society of those years. The boys were offered a profile as hunters, soldiers and racecar drivers; the careers of seamstresses, cooks and mothers were suggested to the girls. The homepage, in fact, is strongly inspired by the set of pages that make up the magazine of Topolino. The Issue section, on the other hand, wants to more markedly transpose the style of the stripes inside the comic.
